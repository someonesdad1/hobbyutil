Introduction

This document's goal is to acquaint others facing shoulder surgery with some of the things they might experience. I'll discuss my routine right shoulder arthroscopic rotator cuff repair which was needed because of an injury. I also had two earlier shoulder surgeries, one for a massive rotator cuff tear of my left shoulder followed by fixing a fracture in the upper humerus of that shoulder 3 months after the rotator cuff repair.

You're probably considering shoulder surgery because of an injury or degradation due to disease or aging. For an injury, you probably went to your family doctor, urgent care, or emergency room and they took x-rays, put you in a sling, and referred you to an orthopedic surgeon. For the chronic condition, you likely went to your family doctor who referred you to an orthopedic surgeon.

The process of finding an orthopedic surgeon deserves some thought. Commonly, you'll just use the person recommended by your family doctor or emergency room. If you have a non-urgent condition, it could be worth your time to work on finding the best surgeon for you in your area.

The surgeon will give you his opinions about your treatment choices. Not all treatments involve surgery, so you need to decide whether you should have the surgery or not.

The major steps you'll go through are:

  • Decide whether to have surgery
  • Preparation for surgery
  • Surgery
  • Recovery from surgery
  • Rehabilitation

These surgeries were done on me while I was around age 70. I'm a fairly active senior and do a lot of do-it-yourself tasks, home and car maintenance, and yard work. Having a non-functional shoulder or significant shoulder pain cramps my lifestyle. For each of the surgeries, my decision was that I would be worse off by not having the surgery.

Consider the experiences in this document as probably typical. Things can change substantially depending on your age, health, medical considerations, etc., so your best strategy is to ask questions of your surgeon and physical therapist.

The burden is on you as the patient to do the necessary research, studying, and asking the right questions. The ultimate decision about your treatment is up to you, not the doctor or therapist.

I feel the best strategy is to find health care professionals whose opinions you trust and follow their guidance, which will be based on their experience.

Hopefully, standards of care are similar enough that what I describe will be close to what you experience.

Overview

I've concluded there are three key things involved in the successful outcome of a shoulder surgery:

  • Surgeon
  • Physical therapist
  • Your commitment to your recovery

Surgeon

Of course you'll want a surgeon who is technically good and compassionate, but it's also important that her coworkers be good too, as they are a team. I spent more time talking with the surgeon's coworkers than the surgeon -- and these coworkers were invaluable. My surgeon, Alice, had a PA (physician's assistant) Carol who assists Alice in all the surgeries. Most of my appointments were with Carol. Carol earned my trust the first time I saw her because of the time she spent with me, the depth of her knowledge, and her explanations of how the fix would work. David, Alice's nurse, answered many questions over the phone and once saved me from making a medication mistake that could have had life-threatening consequences. Alice, Carol, and David also treated my wife's broken humerus (she broke it in a fall similar to the one I had, except her treatment didn't involve surgery) and my wife feels the same way about them that I do.

Physical therapist

You will spend more time with the physical therapist and her staff than anyone else involved in your recovery. I'm fortunate in having a twin sister Debbie who's an outstanding physical therapist. Debbie spent untold hours with me on the phone and in person on a number of visits to her, helping me over numerous recovery humps, particularly with the challenging first two surgeries.

In my initial consultation with the surgeon Alice, I asked her for the best local physical therapist she could recommend, explaining that I had had some not-so-successful treatments in the past with various PTs in our city and I was picky because of knowing Debbie's caliber. Alice recommended Eve and I stayed with Eve for my three surgery recoveries. My good therapy outcomes are testaments to Debbie's and Eve's physical therapy skills.

Commitment

The importance of your commitment is easy to underestimate. You may not appreciate

  • How long it will take for you to recover your functionality.
  • How much work and dedication it will require to do what you are told.
  • How hard it is to stay motivated to do these boring exercises.

By my third surgery (the routine rotator cuff repair), I was absolutely committed to doing exactly what the PT told me, with good form, and with dedication.

Cost

A fourth big thing could be the money involved to pay for the surgery and therapy. I've not addressed the issue of money in this document, but it will be present and may influence your decisions.

Before my first surgery, a neighbor showed me his arm's limitations after his shoulder surgery (he slipped on ice). These limitations came about because he couldn't afford the physical therapy after the surgery, so he stopped the therapy. He wound up with a dissappointing 50% functionality at best, although he had learned to live with it.

For my routine rotator cuff surgery, I had 18 PT appointments over about 4 months (later in my recovery I changed the visits to every 2 weeks). The insurance copayment with the insurance company we had was half of what it was for PT for the previous two surgeries. For the more challenging massive rotator cuff tear and fracture, I had 61 PT appointments over slightly more than a year of therapy. The PT was by far my largest out-of-pocket expense.

You'd be wise to contact your insurance company before the surgery and try to learn what's covered and what's not, what your expenses will be, etc.

Pre-surgery

Diagnosis

I assume there are two situations here: chronic and acute shoulder problems. Chronic means you've had long-term disability or pain in your shoulder. Acute means you probably injured it.

My right shoulder had chronic low-level pain in it over a decade or more. Based on discussions with sister Debbie, I assumed this was slow degradation due to wear, aging, and arthritis. It was never severe enough to cause me to seek a solution. It became more important after I injured it while trying to turn a wrench on a difficult in-ground water valve. I let things sit for a number of months to see if it would improve, but it didn't. I chose to have a surgical repair 6 months later when an MRI showed I had ripped the supraspinatus muscle in half.

The first step of diagnosis is to get a history and to manually examine your shoulder. For an acute injury, it is likely an x-ray of the shoulder will be taken to see whether you have a fracture or not. Many people will go to the emergency room for their shoulder injury, but it's rarely an emergency. You can go to your family doctor's office or an urgent care place, even if it takes a wait of a few days. For a rotator cuff tear or a fracture, it's common to refer you to an orthopedic surgeon.

Your family doctor or the surgeon will first want an MRI to see what's going on if there's no fracture. A fracture will be dealt with first, as it's more urgent than a soft-tissue injury. For a fracture, the surgeon will likely want a CAT scan.

MRI

For soft tissue injuries like rotator cuff problems, MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) is the standard tool for diagnosing the type and extent of problem. From the images the surgeon and his PA have shown me, these images seem to give more than enough information for them to plan their surgical approach in detail, including measuring needed dimensions.

You need to know what to expect from an MRI on your shoulder. You'll lay on your back on a movable table that is inserted into a relatively small tunnel inside a big noisy machine. Your shoulder will be pressed into a fixture to hold it still and a strap will be put across your legs.

Your basic tasks are 1) to lay as still as you can, as any movement destroys the images and 2) try not to panic from any claustrophobic feelings while you're in the tunnel. You're given a rubber bulb to squeeze if you need to stop the test for any reason, so don't worry that you won't be able to get out.

If you do stop the test, realize that the beginning of the process spends some time finding the key anatomical landmarks on your body to reference all the further measurements. If you come out of the tunnel or move, these landmarks can be lost and you have to start all over again.

In the three MRI procedures I have had, two were for shoulder injuries. My three MRIs were memorable because I was not able to lay motionless on my back for long without experiencing growing arm pain caused by my shoulder injuries. I had to lie in that tunnel completely motionless for 20-25 minutes and the pain became significant, to the point where I kept telling the tech to hurry up because it really hurt. It caused significant elbow pain and went all the way down to my hand. I'm supposing it was caused by some pressure on a nerve -- and I've experienced the identical pain from both left and right shoulder injuries on all three MRIs that I've had. However, because I didn't want to have to do the whole thing over again, I toughed it out. For my last MRI, I tried to distract myself by counting off the amount of time the tech told me the next picture was going to take.

For my first MRI, they just put me in the tunnel without telling me anything and I didn't know what to expect. I spent the first half of the time trying to imagine what all the machinery I was hearing was doing. About 2/3 the way through the scan, my brain started to worry about me being trapped and I had some momentary thoughts of claustrophobia. I had to force myself to stop worrying and ignore the pain and tough it out. The tech of my first MRI messed up by not telling me what to expect and not making me wear earphones so she could talk to me every few minutes to tell me the progress. This would have been a big help. I wrote a letter to the hospital suggesting they change their procedures.

For the next MRI, I decided to take a Valium to reduce the probability of panic from claustrophobia. I had a great tech who talked to me and a mirror to allow me to look down the tunnel behind me at the parking lot, so I had no problems at all. This MRI was of my head, but I was waiting for my first shoulder surgery and I still had the bad shoulder pain from laying still.

For my third MRI (for the routine rotator cuff injury) I brought a sedative prescribed by my doctor, but because the MRI folks were ahead of schedule, I came in early and they whisked me in, so I didn't have time to take the pill, as it needs about 30 minutes to start working (I was told not to take it until I got there). But I didn't need it. Once again, the shoulder/arm pain was the dominating factor. I made sure the tech knew to keep communicating with me and updating the status (she was great).

From the experience of these three MRI scans with a shoulder injury, if I have to have another such MRI with a shoulder injury, I will take some pain pills to dull the arm ache from laying still on my back. It's significant enough that I would take at least two 5 mg oxycodone pills, maybe three (the maximum dose) to dull the pain. However, I'd make sure the MRI tech knows I've taken the pain pills and helps me walk to and from the machine, as oxycodone can make you wobbly on your feet. I would also bring a cane with me to help me steady myself. I'd also have someone drive me, as you're not allowed to drive while on such opioids.

The MRI results are sent to the surgeon's office and the PA or surgeon will go over the results with you, showing you the pictures and describing the diagnosis. The pictures will look unfamiliar because you probably haven't seen such things before, but a good PA/doc will help you understand things by using an anatomical model. Usually, the surgeon has enough information to plan his surgical treatment and no further diagnostic information is needed.

Deciding on surgery

A surgical treatment for your shoulder problem may be optional. Even for my fracture, which had a bone rather badly twisted out of position, I could have chosen to spend 6 weeks in a special sling and have it heal without surgery. The surgeon Alice told me she was on the fence about whether to operate or use the sling, so she gave me the necessary information and told me to choose which treatment I wanted. Since I'm an active senior, I decided on the surgery because a key x-ray view looking up the long axis of the humerus showed how much the humeral head had rotated out of position and I felt the best outcome would be if things were put back the way they were before. Both Alice and her PA Carol told me independently after the surgery that they felt I made the correct decision, as they know me and my lifestyle.

Common reasons to have the surgery are that you've lost significant functionality or have ongoing pain. A factoid is that many autopsies have shown that people have torn/degraded rotator cuffs and were able to accommodate and live OK without having things fixed. Because of this, maybe you can get by without that surgery.

You'll want to do your homework to help you decide whether to have the surgery or not. You'll be warned that there's a risk of a bad outcome of the surgery, up to and including death. One of the significant risks is the anesthesia, where many things can go wrong, especially for older folks or people with concomitant medical issues.

For my second rotator cuff surgery, sister Debbie had sent me a review paper on such surgeries and the objective clinical evidence was lukewarm about the efficacy of the surgery. After discussing this paper's results with the surgeon Alice, I decided on the surgery after Alice told me the only justification for having the surgery would be to get rid of chronic pain. That's exactly what I had and is why I chose the third surgery. I'm glad I did, as my chronic pain is gone (and I had had that chronic pain for six months before the surgery -- and it wasn't going away).

There may be some urgency to the surgery even though I stated elsewhere that rotator cuff surgery isn't urgent. For my third surgery, my supraspinatus muscle was completely torn and Carol, the surgeon's PA, commented that the quality of surgical repair outcomes can drop the longer you wait. I had waited 6 months for the surgery and Carol commented that waiting 9 months to a year after the injury could mean a poorer outcome or possibly a lost surgical window. For the first surgery (massive rotator cuff tear), Alice the surgeon told me it was a significant injury and its repair was pretty urgent.

You'll experience significant pain from the surgery and it will likely be months before you can start doing even relatively simple things again. If you make your living doing physical work, it can be a lot longer before you're ready to perform your job again. Ask your surgeon how long you'll be off work, as this will stress you financially because you may not have enough income. You won't be able to drive for 4 to 6 weeks. You'll need someone to help you with some daily activities like getting dressed, showering, etc., at least in the first week or two. You also need to worry about falling, which can quickly undo the repairs done in surgery.

After your surgery, simple everyday tasks can become difficult to do with one painful arm in a sling. For example, pulling up your pants and tightening your belt will suddenly become a tough thing to do. Putting on a tee shirt in the first week after surgery will be surprisingly hard (there's a trick to it I'll mention later). If you injure your dominant arm, you'll have to learn to wipe yourself with your other arm after going to the bathroom, something you may have never done nor thought about. However, you'll learn to accommodate and you will get through it, so don't reject the surgery for such reasons.

Selecting a surgeon and therapist

For my first rotator cuff tear, my family doctor Bob referred me to an orthopedic surgeon who had operated on Bob's knee. Unfortunately, an appointment with him was more than three months out. A few days after the injury, I was on a trip out of state and visited with my sister Debbie. She spent an hour and a half going over the list of orthopedic surgeons in my area that my insurance would cover. There were about 40 of them and most she weeded out pretty quickly. I was left with a list of about 4 surgeons Debbie considered the best to choose from. Debbie's experience with thousands of shoulder cases over her career was invaluable in this selection.

By far the first choice on Debbie's list was Alice, both because of the information on Alice's website and the fact that Alice had trained at a surgical institute headed by a surgeon that Debbie knew and had worked with in the 1970's.

After returning from visiting Debbie, I talked with Jimmy, one of Bob's nurses and explained that 3 months is a pretty long wait. I mentioned that Alice was number one on my list and Jimmy said he'd call the surgeon's office. When Jimmy called me back, he said they had an opening that afternoon because of a cancellation. My first appointment was with Carol, Alice's PA, and Carol went over in detail what the diagnosis was from the MRI and what and how they would fix it. I was very impressed with Carol and didn't even see the surgeon Alice. Later that evening around 8 pm, the surgeon Alice called me and talked about the surgery for about 20 minutes. Alice felt the surgery was pretty urgent because it was a massive tear. There was a medical concern about the surgery and Alice called my family doctor Bob to discuss it. Bob later told me that he was impressed by Alice's concern and medical knowledge, as they spent perhaps 15 minutes on the phone. Bob was so impressed with Alice that now he sends all his shoulder patients to Alice -- and Bob told me his simple reason is "they all get better".

Unfortunately, most folks won't have an expert resource like I did to help with selecting a surgeon. You'll probably have to rely on the referral from your family doctor or emergency room. A creative person might be able to find other ways to learn more about the different surgeons available. For example, I worked for decades with a friend whose wife was a surgical nurse at a local hospital. When I had a leg fracture in the 1980's that needed a surgical repair, I bounced the name of the surgeon off my friend, who asked his wife about the surgeon's reputation and I got useful feedback that helped me with the decision.

You may have a friend who is associated with someone in the health care industry. Use such contacts if you can, as getting first-hand knowledge can be helpful in making a decision. You won't get such information by looking at surgeon's websites, as you'll only see carefully-written marketing information).

Something I know now is that I would gladly pay someone for their time for their professional advice to help me with this surgical decision/selection. For example, I'd contact my PT Eve and pay her for some of her time to help me decide on a surgeon. The PTs will know and have worked with a number of surgeons and may be willing to give you objective data or at least make a recommendation. However, there's a chance that they won't be perfectly frank, as a bad recommendation might get back to a surgeon and affect that PT's future referrals.

Another idea could be to try to schedule 10 minutes with an orthopedic surgeon to ask for advice on a surgeon to select for your surgery, specifically ruling the person who you're talking to out as a candidate surgeon. Present them with the question of who they would ask to operate on their spouse or child. I don't know if such things are done, but it can't hurt to ask. It might be worth your time to wander around the orthopedic ward at a local hospital, asking folks for their opinions. If you're up-front, it's likely not unethical, but some folks may not be willing to discuss such things. Still, you'll probably have more useful information than blindly relying on a referral from the emergency room folks or looking surgeons up on the web or in the phone book.

Gentle pressure

Because it can be hard getting an appointment with a surgeon, I suggest gentle pressure. A good tactic (at least before COVID) is to go into the surgeon's office and talk to the scheduler in person if you can. Get to know them and offer to be available for a cancellation at any time. Of course, this is much easier for someone like myself who is retired. But if you make it a priority, you may be able to get in quickly for your first appointment. After that, the surgeon's office will be able to accommodate you with the follow-up appointments, as you're now in their rotation. Use your best people skills with the scheduler -- you want this person on your side.

As an example, I needed some relatively minor abdominal surgery about 6 months after my last shoulder surgery and my shoulder surgeon Alice recommended a surgeon in her building. At my yearly physical, I had my family doctor Bob make a referral to this surgeon. The surgeon's office called me and said the first appointment was quite a few months out. I chatted with the woman making the appointments and we joked about a number of things, as she had a good sense of humor. I explained I was retired and could get to their office in less than 15 minutes on short notice. In a few days, she called me back with a cancellation, but luck would have it that it was right in the middle of my next PT appointment. A few days later, she called again and I got another appointment a week later, also due to a cancellation. I went from many months of waiting for surgery to less than two weeks.

Preparation for surgery

Your primary source of information on how to prepare for the surgery will be the surgeon's office. They'll provide you with printed information that tells you the things you'll need to do. Read such instructions carefully. One of the most important is to avoid food on the day of surgery, as food in your stomach under anesthesia can result in vomiting, which might quickly turn into a life-threatening emergency if you breathe the vomited material into your lungs. Another danger is infection, so follow the cleaning and antiseptic instructions carefully. Thirdly, pay particular attention to what they tell you about any medications you're taking.

One of the best preparations should be a careful medical history. For my first surgery, a nurse at the hospital spent nearly an hour and a half taking a careful history, asking me lots of questions, and writing it all down. I don't believe any of this information was needed in the subsequent surgeries, but if there was a problem, it could have been incredibly valuable to the doctor on the spot if there was an emergency they had to respond to. I was grateful for the hard work and thoroughness of this nurse taking this history.

For my third surgery, I was sent to a hospital in another town for a thorough evaluation by a PA. This PA asked me a lot of questions, took another careful history, and had the results reviewed by an on-staff internal medicine doctor. Their focus was on evaluating my risks to anesthesia and included giving me an electrocardiogram. I was quite impressed with the abilities and people skills of this PA.

The folks at the hospital gave me a surgical prep kit that contained a soap to wash the shoulder with the night before and the morning of the surgery. After the morning shower, I also swabbed a disinfectant over the surgical area. Follow these instructions carefully, as they'll help avoid an infection.

You'll also be instructed on what medications to take and not take the day of surgery. Again, follow these instructions carefully to make sure you're doing what was asked of you. Not following the instructions nor telling the medical folks you've not followed them is foolish and can put you at unnecessary risk.

For my third surgery, I was required to have a COVID19 test two days before the surgery.

You may be sent to a medical supply place before your shoulder surgery to be fitted for a sling. Since you may spend 6 weeks in this sling, you'll want to make sure it's fitted properly and you've gotten instruction in its use. If you can, spend a day or two in it before the surgery because this will give you some hints about how your life will be changed by the shoulder surgery. Find out what it's like to try to pull up your pants with one hand, get dressed (putting on socks with one hand is challenging, but you can learn to do it), and do other common daily activities. Even if you do this practice, it won't be realistic because you won't be able to simulate the pain you'll experience when you accidentally move the arm that has had the surgery.

Surgery

You'll be given a time to appear at the hospital and where to go inside the hospital. You'll first go through the paperwork, arrange for payment, and go through multiple checks to identify you. You'll be given a wrist-band that identifies you. Don't be surprised by the number of times that this will be scanned and that you're asked who you are, as these procedures have evolved from stupid mistakes made in hospitals, such as operating on the wrong limb or doing the wrong procedure.

You'll wait to go into the surgical prep area. When they can accept you, you'll remove all clothes and put on a hospital gown. You'll need to remove contact lenses, hearing aids, dental accessories, jewelry, etc. The nurse will take vital signs and get you ready for the surgery. Inserting an IV is a typical task. I had an incredibly nice and competent nurse for my first shoulder surgery and we joked around a lot. Sadly, for my next rotator cuff repair two years later, she had retired and everyone missed her terribly because she was such a wonderful person.

You'll be asked to sign a consent form for the surgery. It will explicitly state that the risks can include death. These things are of course to minimize the malpractice risks of the hospital and doctors. If you're concerned about such things, you may want to get a copy of the form you'll be asked to sign ahead of time.

An anesthesiologist will talk with you and give you a quick examination (e.g., listen to your heart and lungs). They will ask you about what type of anesthesia you'd like to have. Ask them to explain your choices and the benefits/risks associated with each one. For each of my rotator cuff repairs, I asked for a nerve block (based on the advice of my sister Debbie), which is a simple injection into a nerve nexus in your neck. Both of mine lasted for about 24 hours, which was great, but I was told in the two years since my first one there are ones that now can last even longer. Unfortunately, the anesthesiologist who started my treatment and explained this wasn't the one who gave me the injection (it was right at shift change), so I only got the one day numbing even though I asked for the multiple day numbing. Thus, don't be shy about verifying you're getting what you asked for.

To give the nerve block, the anesthesiologist alerts the nurse to inject a quick-acting sedative into your IV. As soon as you feel the first sign of wooziness from this injection, the anesthesiologist will use an ultrasound device to find the proper area for injection in your neck. You'll feel a slight prick from the injection, but it's over with quickly and totally ignorable. Interestingly, for both my left and right shoulder surgeries, the injection was given on the right side of my neck.

For my fracture surgery, I wasn't given any choices about the anesthesia.

Your surgeon will visit you just before your surgery and answer any last-minute questions. He'll write on the shoulder to be operated on after asking you which shoulder is to be operated on.

When it's time for your surgery, you'll be wheeled into the operating room. I was conscious and asked a lot of questions for each surgery because I'm curious about such things. I still remember the people in the operating room and joking around with them. They will ask you to help them move you off the gurney onto the operating table, so do the best you can with your injured arm.

I was a bit sedated when I was wheeled to the operating room for my first surgery and it's a little hazy, but I was wide awake for the next two surgeries. For my fracture surgery, I was surprised by the number of people in the operating room (perhaps six people) and I joked with the x-ray tech who was wearing a gaudy Halloween lead apron, as it was October 9th. I particularly remember the anesthesiology nurse starting an injection into my IV to put me under and it was surprisingly painful, causing my whole right arm to burn. I complained about it and he apologized, but a few seconds later I lost consciousness. For my third surgery, I commented on this to one of the folks in the operating room and even though they weren't going to use that particular method with me, they said that they now have a way of blocking out that initial pain. Thus, the processes keep improving, as they should. I again joked around with the folks in the operating room and I remember talking to Carol, the surgeon's PA, as she was there helping get things ready.

Soon, you'll be unconscious from the anesthesia and the next thing you'll be aware of is waking up in the recovery room.

Post-surgery

Recovering from surgery

After my first rotator cuff surgery, my first conscious thought when waking up was that I was laying on my back and that someone had put a cell phone down my throat. I tried to say "Get this cell phone out of my throat!", but couldn't speak. In a few seconds after waking up, it was gone. It was an intubation tube stuck into my trachea, as they keep you intubated through the surgery. I coughed a few times and then started talking like I had a frog in my throat.

The nurse attending me (call him Jerry) said I instantly came awake because he had just inserted a Foley catheter in me, which is used to drain urine. This was after 3 pm and I hadn't peed since early that morning, so my bladder was full. This was in phase 1 recovery and they monitor you fairly closely. When you're stable, they'll move you to phase 2 recovery where your spouse can visit with you. This was about 15 to 20 minutes after regaining consciousness. A nurse's assistant, Karl, was also present and he told me that he had just learned how to put a patient's contact lenses in. Karl put my lenses in and I was much happier, as I could see things again (I'm very nearsighted). Karl and Jerry followed me into phase 2 recovery and my wife came in to be with me. She took pictures of me with her cell phone and I looked like I had the mumps, as I was still quite swollen from the fluids injected by the surgeon to make the surgical area easier to see by spreading the tissues.

I wanted the Foley catheter out as soon as possible, as it's uncomfortable. Karl pulled it out and it only takes a few seconds, but it made me yelp in pain. What I didn't realize was that my urethra was going to hurt for the next few days every time I had to pee. As I was riding the wheelchair out to leave the hospital, I stopped in the bathroom and peed. Oh, boy, did it hurt. I definitely did not want to pee and have this pain over the next couple of days, but of course you have no choice (and I had blood in my urine for a couple of days). At about day 3 the pain went away and I was ecstatic for two or three days every time I went in to pee, as there was no pain. This experience taught me to pee immediately before I was wheeled into the operating room for the next two shoulder surgeries, as I didn't want a catheter again.

For my two rotator cuff surgeries, I came to the hospital in the morning and left for home in the afternoon, so they were done on an outpatient basis. For the fracture surgery with the larger incision, it's typical that they'll want you to stay overnight and they'll release you in the morning. When you stay overnight, a nurse will come in and monitor things every few hours, so you won't get a great night's sleep. An important detail is to always ring for the nurse if you want to go to the bathroom so they can help you. No one wants to see you fall after your surgery.

Before being released from the hospital after the fracture surgery, I had an enthusiastic physical therapist visit me and give me tips about dealing with the surgery. He had me walk around the ward with a "leash" on so he could help me avoid a fall, as I was on opioid pain medications at this point and they can make you wobbly. He recommended that I get a cane to help with walking over the next week or two, so we stopped at a store on the way home and bought one. It's an excellent tool and I used it for a number of weeks, even after stopping taking the pain medications. I also used it many times for helping with external rotation stretching after my third surgery.

After the first rotator cuff surgery, I thought I'd be hungry after the surgery because I hadn't eaten anything since the dinner the previous day (nearly 24 hours before), but I wasn't hungry when I left the hospital.

After the surgeries with the nerve blocks, I kept feeling a piece of metal that must have been on the sling hitting me in the stomach. I'd lift the loose shirt I was wearing and be surprised that it was my thumb that kept hitting me. My surgical arm was completely paralyzed and had no feeling at all -- I could pinch it and not feel a thing. That's how effective a nerve block is.

You'll probably have a cough from being intubated. After my third surgery, I had a cough for a week or two (I'd had the COVID infection 6 months before and this might have been related).

Surgical pain

Once the anesthesia wears off, your surgical area is going to hurt. Many people are surprised by the pain levels and sometimes their surgeon doesn't prepare them well enough to expect such pain levels. Your surgeon probably prescribed a narcotic pain medication like oxycodone for you. You need to understand exactly how to use these medicines, as they can be addictive and taking too much can be fatal.

Ideally, you want to be on top of the pain and start your medications before the onset of the pain. But this is difficult to predict. My nerve blocks lasted about 24 hours. I had asked for a multi-day nerve block for my second rotator cuff surgery, but the shift change had an anesthesiologist change and the second doctor missed the first doctor's promise. Thus, after about 24 hours I started to feel pain and was a bit mystified by it, but then decided to start taking the pain medications. I started off with taking three 5 mg oxycodone tablets every 4 hours, the maximum dose. For all three surgeries, I took 15 mg of oxycodone every 4 hours for about 12 hours. At around 11 pm the day after the surgery I no longer needed the pain medications. For my first surgery, however, I took the oxycodone at night for the next two nights, as it helped with me getting to sleep.

Besides the risks of getting addicted or overdosing, there are two other problems with opioids. First, they can cause constipation. Your doctor's instructions will warn you about this and they'll recommend a stool softener pill. I recommend going farther than that and taking a good dose of something like Metamucil every day. You'll also want a high fiber diet. A large salad for lunch with beans and lots of vegetables works well for me.

Second, the opioids can cause nausea. This can come on quickly with no warning. Your doctor should also have prescribed an anti-nausea pill like Ondansetron. This is a small pill you dissolve under your tongue and they work surprisingly quickly. I had an onset of nausea a few times after the surgeries and these anti-nausea pills are nearly magical. I remember a leg surgery in the 1980's and being given morphine for it -- and throwing up violently at midnight in the hospital room from this morphine.

My shoulder surgery pain levels subjectively behave in about the following way:

  • Highest pain level was 24-30 hours after the surgery. On a scale of 1 to 10, I rate the worst pain levels around 6.
  • After about 1 week, the pain levels had dropped by about a factor of 2.
  • After 2 weeks, another drop of about 2.
  • After 3-4 weeks, there was no pain unless I moved my arm.

The first week after the surgery is the hardest, the second week is less hard but still painful. You have to grit your teeth and just make it through the sleeplessness and ongoing pain. The pain will drop over time, but it does seem to go slowly. Just make your mind up to get through things one day at a time.

Watching movies or having an involved conversation with someone are good to help you get your mind off your pain. I found pacing up and down good also -- and the gentle bumping of the arm while you're walking seemed to help, somewhat counter-intuitively.

It is typical for me that I am unable to do the simple passive stretching recommended after the surgery because of the pain. The PA told me a week after the surgery that I could start pendulum, external rotation, and lay on the back and raise my arm over my head (forward flexion). But it was too painful to do and it was a couple of weeks before I was able to get into doing them.

Sleeping

Your shoulder surgery is going to interrupt your sleep, possibly for months. I was not able to lay flat on my back for months after the surgery because I'd develop a nerve pain in my arm (described when I had to have the MRI procedures). I'd instead try to sleep in my recliner in the living room -- and often I'd have to sleep sitting up because I couldn't recline at all. This meant I rarely was able to sleep for more than half an hour without waking up. It was also complicated by being an old geezer who has to get up and pee numerous times a night.

For my first two surgeries, I believe I slept in my recliner in the living room for about 2 months after the surgery. In my recliner, I couldn't sleep on my side nor fully stretch out flat. You'll want to periodically try to sleep in your bed again, as it will slowly get more comfortable over time. For my third surgery, I only needed to sleep in the recliner for a month, then went back to my bed. It was another two months before I was able to sleep comfortably on my surgical shoulder.

Blowing out the repair

You're at risk for multiple months after the rotator cuff surgery from "blowing out" the surgical repair. This means injuring the shoulder again before the tissues have healed sufficiently. The surgeon's office will warn you about this, as surgeons/PAs and PTs see this happen fairly frequently. The strategic reasons to avoid it are 1) you undo all the hard work of the surgeon, your PT, and your commitment and 2) after such a blow-out, the surgical re-repair is typically nowhere near as good as the initial repair.

Clearly, an accident like a fall can easily blow out the surgery. What you won't appreciate, however, is that less-obvious things can do it too. The best strategy to avoid a blow out is to keep your arm in your sling, properly fitted, at all times. A non-obvious thing that can damage the surgical repair is dropping something and instinctively grabbing at it with the surgical arm. It can happen so fast you won't be aware of it. If your arm is in a sling, it's more likely the sling will help prevent this.

I often opened the sling while in my recliner to be able to rest my arm in a different position for comfort. After a careful caution by the PA Carol after my third surgery about keeping my arm in the sling, I mentally forced myself to put the sling back on EVERY time I got out of my recliner, even if I was just going a couple of steps away.

Here's another non-obvious source of blow out: if you're sleeping in your recliner and having a dream, you may involuntarily move your surgical arm violently during the dream. I never had this happen, but I've heard of it happening. Because of this, I tried to sleep in the recliner with the sling on, but this was often difficult because of the nerve pain.

The risk of a blow out exists for quite a while, especially for tendon repairs which take a long time to heal because of poorer blood supply. There's no magical date where the blow out risk goes from high to low. I suspect the risk is significant for 4 to 6 months for a routine repair and 6 months to a year or more for a complicated repair. Ask your surgeon, as she'll know best.

Physical Therapy

In my experience, the physical therapy phase is the hardest part of a shoulder surgery because

  • It requires significant commitment and faith in the PT's judgment
  • It requires much more time than you think
  • It's a boring repetitive task that needs to be done every day

Because it's the hardest part, I'll spend the most time on it.

The initial stretching and range of motion tasks had to be done twice daily for the first three months -- and this gets old pretty quickly. It's easy to find excuses not to do your assigned tasks.

A physical therapist is a person trained in a variety of treatments and methods of helping people regain motor skills after injury or surgery (this is a limited definition suitable for this document, although PTs can have many other skills). A PT's training is minimally a four year college degree, although doctorate-level training is now the preferred level of training as stated by the PT's professional organization. PT offices also employ physical therapy assistants (they typically have two years of college training) who will also treat you under the supervision of a PT.

My twin sister Debbie is a doctoral-level physical therapist. She has seen thousands of shoulder patients over her career and was invaluable to me in many phone calls and visits while recovering from my surgeries. She has had shoulder surgery herself, so she intimately knows what shoulder patients go through.

Your surgeon or her PA will issue a prescription for physical therapy, which will define the protocols the physical therapist follows during your treatment. The PT will be careful to adhere to the surgeon's instructions. A few days to a few weeks after your surgery you'll start seeing a physical therapist. Your PT treatment will probably go through the following stages:

  • Pain control: If you have uncontrolled pain, you won't do stretching or exercises, so pain needs to be dealt with first. Your PT will have numerous techniques to try to help you with pain.
  • Passive range of motion: Because you have been in a sling, your tendons and muscles will tighten up and atrophy from lack of use. To avoid a frozen shoulder, the first focus will be on passive stretching (passive means you don't use the injured shoulder's muscles) to regain lost range of motion (ROM).
  • AAROM: As you progress, you'll use Active-Assisted Range of Motion techniques, which means using your good arm to e.g. help move your injured arm, along with partial use of the injured arm's muscles. You'll slowly transition to active movement, which involves using the injured arm's muscles for the movement. This needs to be done carefully to avoid overstressing the repaired muscles/tendons.
  • Neuromuscular retraining: You may have learned alternative muscle movements that accommodate your injury. These need to be unlearned, as they can interfere with the rehabilitation of the muscles you should be using. Your PT will give you specific exercises and instructions on what to do. Watching yourself exercise in a mirror is an excellent feedback technique to verify you're doing things correctly, but the best feedback comes from the PT or PT assistant watching you do your stretching and exercises.
  • Strengthening: the last phase of rehabilitation is to regain the lost muscle strength in the targeted muscles. This can take a long time, sometimes over a year. A common focus of the PT is to ensure you regain the needed strength and functionality to do your common daily tasks.

For my rehab, it was typical that the first 6 weeks was focused on stretching techniques to regain range of motion. The next 6 weeks used active-assisted range of motion techniques and isometric exercises (it's still a bit early to be straining the sutures in your shoulder). Strength training then started after 12 weeks of PT -- and started gently.

PTs use many techniques to aid recovery. I have had a number of different treatments applied to me during a PT appointment and I have tried to objectively evaluate them for effectiveness, at least for my treatments. I will give my opinions on the treatments I have had and their effectiveness, but realize that these things can be subtle and a technique that I didn't find effective for me might work well for you. Thus, approach things with an open mind and look for objective benefits.

PTs use heating and cooling pads to warm and cool tissues. I found the heating pads sometimes useful during my 8 minute warm-up period on the "shoulder bicycle". The chilled cooling pads can be nearly magical, as they can help relieve soreness and discomfort after stretching or exercises. Learn to apply them properly and you can use them as needed at home (the key is to not let your skin temperature get too low). Early in my PT appointments I'd be treated with a cold pad, but I quickly decided that was something I could do myself at home and there was no need to "waste" valuable PT time with something I could do myself.

Two other treatments I've had are electrostimulation and ultrasonic energy deposited in the injured tissues. Electrostimulation involves small currents (on the order of 5 microamperes) being passed through muscles to make them involuntarily contract. I've had it applied somewhere between 10 and 15 times and I never could feel any benefit from the treatment, so I asked that it not be used anymore. Debbie said there's no strong clinical evidence that it helps everyone, though some patients feel it's beneficial. She said she used it on herself for her shoulder and wrist surgery therapy.

The principle of ultrasound seems to be that the sonic energy is absorbed in the tissues, causing gentle heating (and maybe some physical movement, although it will likely be tiny). This supposedly helps improve blood flow which should aid healing. Physically, the effect is probably small and I could never feel any benefit from it, so I chose not to use it after a few trials. An FDA site says that ultrasound can raise tissue temperatures perhaps 3 to 5 degrees C.

PTs will massage an incision area to help reduce the scar tissue. They can show you how to do this yourself.

The skill I most value in the PT and PT assistants is their ability to manipulate your arm to determine your range of motion and their various techniques of massaging and digging their fingers into trigger points. They know all about your shoulder's state after handling it for a minute or two and feeling the elasticity ("end points") and how the muscles/joints move. They'll use a rotating angle gauge (called a goniometer) to measure your range of motion and note it on your chart. I always had them write the numbers down for both my notebook and to send to my sister Debbie to keep her apprised of my progress.

Digging their fingers into trigger points is used by the PT to help relax your shoulder muscles and improve range of motion. The technique is borderline uncomfortable while it's being done, but my shoulder always felt looser and more relaxed as I left the PT's office. Interestingly, Eve told me the insurance companies seem to value the exercise equipment more than these hands-on skills of the PT. Eve spent a lot of time during my first 10 or so PT appointments digging into my scapula, trying to get it to free up. It tightens up significantly while your arm is in that sling. This is one of the locations that's hard for the patient themselves to treat.

After the desired range of motion is achieved, the therapy will transition into regaining lost strength and getting rid of learned accommodations. This will take more time than you think and will probably proceed slowly per the surgeon's protocol. Eve knew that I was feeling able to do a bit more than she was assigning me, but she very reasonably told me that both she and I would be fools to "blow out" the surgeon's careful repair by starting an exercise a week or two early. My risk would be that months of twice-daily PT work would be lost and a second surgical repair is never as effective as the first. Her risk would be losing professional credibility with the surgeon. Thus, we both stood to lose a lot, so we wisely stayed with the conservative progression. I don't regret it in the least. I saw Alice the surgeon a few weeks later for my last appointment with her (4 months after the surgery) and she was pleased with my progress.

Strengthening will start with isometric exercises and progress into using big rubber bands called Therabands and using weights. Both are useful. I like Therabands because you can adjust the level of exercise by moving slightly. Thus, a typical exercise is to e.g. do 40 "rowing motions" in two sets of 20 with a 30 second rest between the sets. Your goal is to start reaching muscle fatigue around the count of 16 or 18, making it tough to get those last few movements. If you're not fatigued enough, step away to stretch the bands slightly more and you'll get more fatigue. With weights, the only way to increase the fatigue without increasing the weight is to increase the number of repetitions or slow the movements down. Follow the PT's advice on which bands and weights to use and be careful not to overdo things.

Part of the art of the physical therapist is to 1) find methods of treatment, stretching, and exercise that work for the patient and 2) that the patient will do as instructed. For example, Eve showed me 3 or 4 different ways of doing a particular stretching motion for my shoulder and she told me to pick the one that worked best for me. A constant barrier to successful PT treatment is that the patients find excuses not to do their stretches and exercises. I understand this perfectly, as it's time-consuming and monotonous -- and we'd rather be doing something else. However, by my third surgery, I was committed to doing my stretches and exercises exactly as I was instructed because I know that Eve knows her stuff and knows what's best for me while following the surgeon's protocol. I did my stretches/exercises twice a day for 3 months, every day, only missing one afternoon because I was outside working on chores. Around 3 months, my exercises with weights and Therabands changed to once per day, which is easy to get done first thing in the morning. If you're doing your exercises right, you'll find your shoulder will likely be sore, but pay attention to how things become easier week by week. Improvement is slow, but it's noticeable.

A good contrast is to do your exercise or stretch with your other (presumably good) arm periodically. This demonstrates how much further you need to progress. It's also helpful to show you your progress.

Your PT or PT assistant can immediately see if you're doing your work, from a variety of tells. They can put their hands on your shoulder and tell from the tone of the muscles. Watching you exercise tells them a lot at a glance, if for no other reason than how facile the exercise is for you, indicating you've done it multiple times. Finally, manipulating your shoulder in soft tissue work and assessing the range of motion and end points is the most telling of all. Don't think that you can fool them, as you can't.

After my third surgery and PT, my wife and I camped in another state with my sister Debbie and her husband. My sister had me take my shirt off and evaluated me at her campsite for about 10 minutes, both pushing with me resisting with both arms for comparisons as well as just visually inspecting my movements. Then she said "A+, well done!" -- which was high praise from her indeed, as she has high standards. This was the ultimate benefit of following Eve's instructions exactly and diligently -- a normally working shoulder again. During my recovery, I mentioned a number of times to Debbie about my commitment and I'd get back comments like "Imagine that, a patient that does what they're told to." This was a clue that PTs can have a hard time convincing patients what needs to be done and to follow their treatment plan. But I can honestly say they really do know what they're doing. Find the best PT you can and follow their instructions exactly.

Form

It's easy to get sloppy when doing your exercises or stretches, particularly because they are a boring repetitive task. Often, we hurry through them to get them over with. The faster you do them, the crappier your form.

Another problem, particularly for those with testosterone coursing through their system, is the attitude that if a little is good, then more must be better. I fell to this problem during the PT for my third surgery, reasoning that I wasn't getting tired enough with my weights, so I increased the weights.

Both my sister Debbie and the PT assistant Frank were concerned about my descriptions of the weights I was using, much larger than they felt appropriate for the stage I was at. Debbie cautioned me to have a critical evaluation of all this at my next PT appointment and Frank watched me do my exercises. Frank gave me some important education in both maintaining proper form and posture while doing my PT tasks along with how to move the weights. Sounds simple, but I was rushing through them. Frank had me slow way down and feel the exercise I was getting by moving slowly. I got a thorough workout with half the weight. In fact, for the first three days after this review I only did 20 repetitions with the halved weight, then changed to 30 for the next three days and planned to go to 40 on 7 days later. However, on the sixth day, the exercises felt good enough that I did 40 repetitions a day early.

A key principle I had missed was that both Debbie and Frank wanted me doing the movements with the weight over my full range of motion. With the heavier weight, I couldn't move as far and in fact would have been losing strength over my full range of motion.

One of the best feedback techniques besides being watched by a PT or her assistant is to watch yourself in a mirror. I also found to get good form that it was helpful to back up to a wall and touch my head and shoulders to the wall, telling me with tactile feedback that I was using good posture. Another thing I had to focus on was to consciously make sure I wasn't shrugging my shoulders, something you will do unconsciously.

You can use parallax to tell when you're moving when you shouldn't be. For example, when I do Theraband exercises, I use the edge of a door jamb in relation to a wall feature farther away. If these two things move with respect to each other when I'm exercising, I'm moving my body in an inappropriate way.

Discouragement

It's not hard to get discouraged by the long rehabilitation time and constant pain of a shoulder surgery. My first rotator cuff surgery was to fix a massive tear where essentially all the muscles in my left shoulder were torn and my biceps tendon was broken. Alice the surgeon said it was a challenging arthroscopic repair that took 3 hours. She placed 7 posts into the bones to provide suture anchors for the repair of the torn tissues. Three months after this surgery (end of September 2018) I accidentally fell on the arm after getting cramps in both of my calf muscles, fracturing my left humerus. I chose to have ORIF (open reduction internal fixation) surgery that realigned the fractured bone and used metal plates and 13 screws to fix the pieces back together again so they could heal. This needed a 125 mm shoulder incision and the biceps tendon repair needed a 25 mm incision. During my surgical prep for the fracture, the anesthesiologist who handled me during the first surgery was next to me and he remembered my surgery from three months before, commenting that he was quite concerned about my breathing after the surgery was over because of all the fluids needed to be used to inflate the surgical areas for good viewing of the complicated surgery (I mentioned above I looked like I had the mumps in the recovery room).

The fracture and its recovery of course interrupted the recovery of the first rotator cuff surgery and I was again in a sling for 6 weeks. My PT for this extended to a bit more than a year after the first surgery. Eve commented that this double injury/surgery was one of the more challenging cases she has managed.

Around 7 months of PT after the fracture, I was still experiencing significant shoulder pain and I had slacked off on my PT exercises, reasoning that it couldn't be good to be doing the exercises if it still hurt. Around this time I visited my sister Debbie and she said I had unfortunately regressed since she had last seen me a few months before. If I was her patient, she would encourage me to adopt an intense and dedicated PT program for 2 months to recover what I had lost. She said I'd have about 50% shoulder functionality if I stopped where I was. With an intense 2 month program, I might be able to get around 80% functionality.

She explained that I was long past the time the tissues should have healed, which is considered to be about 3 months. She had me read a book called "Explain Pain" by David Butler and Lorimer Moseley, Noigroup Publications, Australia, 2003. This pain was a learned response because my brain was trying to protect my body from the multiple traumas (four: tear, fracture, and two surgeries). If I wanted to regain about an 80% shoulder, I was going to have to commit to this intensive 2-month repeat of PT to get past this point, strengthen my arm, and regain some of the functionality I had lost because I had slacked off on my PT program. I chose to make the commitment, going to PT appointments with Eve or her assistant twice per week.

One of the key things I learned was I had to tell my brain over and over again that this pain is not real. I literally had to ignore the pain -- which is hard to do. Sure enough, over time the pain went away. This was a most surprising and unexpected lesson. Now that I'm writing this more than two years later, I would say that I regained more than 90% of my left shoulder functionality, maybe nearly all of it (maybe not quite all of the pre-injury strength, however). This is both a testament to the surgeon Alice's and her PA Carol's skills and the PTs' skills (Debbie and Eve). Also, don't underestimate the importance of you making that commitment and sticking with it.

Tips

Living with one arm

With a shoulder injury, you suddenly have to figure out how to do things with (mostly) one arm. With a torn rotator cuff before surgery, you'll probably find that there are some movements you can't do at all, either because of loss of function or pain. After surgery or a fracture is repaired, you won't want to move your injured arm because of the pain. What used to be simple isn't simple anymore.

Many of the things you do with two hands can be done with one, although you do them more slowly. Others, like opening a ziplock bag, require shoulder muscles of two arms and are things you won't be able to do. You either find a way to accommodate (e.g., use your teeth) or ask someone to do it for you.

Pulling up your pants after using the toilet can be challenging, but you'll find you can do it with one arm. The thing you won't be able to do is tighten a regular belt, so you may want to find pants with an elastic waist. A few weeks to a month or so after your surgery, you may find that you can tie a drawstring fairly easily, so that's another choice. The type of belts with two "D" rings is one type you can tighten and loosen with one hand. Ask your PT for advice, as they'll have dealt with these things.

As time goes on, you'll find that you can utilize the injured shoulder a little more. For example, unscrewing a lid from a container that has already been open may only require the injured arm to hold things lightly on the counter, and this suddenly becomes doable. Opening a sealed container might take lots more time because it requires more force.

Getting dressed is one of the daily challenges. For a few days after my shoulder fracture, I couldn't bend over and could barely even sit down because of the pain, so I had to have my wife help me dress (I had to live with the fracture for 10 days before it could be surgically repaired). For the first week after rotator cuff surgery, I wore shirts my wife had slit up the seam on the injured shoulder side and these were easy to put on with one hand (buttoning with one hand was a little harder, but it can be done). Putting on underpants or pants can be a bit of a challenge -- a good tactic is to sit down and just slowly work things on with one hand. Getting elasticized socks on with one hand is tricky, but can be done if you put your good hand inside the sock and then use it to help start the toes.

Eve showed me a trick for putting a tee shirt on: hang the injured arm down in the pendulum position and slide the corresponding sleeve up over the injured arm with your good arm. Once you've got it over the injured shoulder, you can pop your head through, then the other arm (sit down if you're still wobbly or on pain medications). Then put your sling on over the tee shirt. As soon as I learned this, I quit wearing the shirts with the slit seam, as I liked the tee shirts better.

My first rotator cuff surgery was near the end of June, so my recovery was during warm summer weather. My fracture surgery was near the beginning of October, so I needed to wear a pile jacket for more warmth. I'd stick my right (good) arm through its sleeve, then use my teeth to pull the elasticized cuff over my hand. I'd then drape the rest of the jacket over the injured left arm and sling (this requires a bit of fiddling). I had my wife pull the unused sleeve inside the jacket so it wouldn't catch on things. I wasn't able to zip the jacket up, so colder weather would require a different method (again, ask your PT for advice on what to do). For going out in the rain, consider a poncho, as it should be easy to put on with one hand.

In March of 2019, my wife accidentally tripped on something in the garage and her fall broke the upper part of her humerus (the same fracture I had). She was first seen at an urgent care facility on a Saturday evening at around 7:30 pm. They put her in a cheap ambidextrous sling to hold her arm steady, but it wasn't an overly comfortable sling. We got in to see my surgeon and her PA the following Monday and they wanted a CAT scan to go along with the urgent care x-rays. After the scan, my wife was able to get a shoulder sling similar to the one I had for my shoulder. These were more comfortable than the cheap slings. A woman's breast can be compressed by the folded arm in the sling, so she'll want to ask about ways to accommodate this additional inconvenience, particularly since she won't be able to put on a bra. The PA and surgeon decided the best treatment for her was 6 weeks in a sling, as there wasn't significant displacement of the bone. She was out of her sling in 6 weeks and started physical therapy with the same PT I used.

PT in the morning

Doing your PT stretching and exercising is often a boring, repetitive task -- especially if you have to do things twice a day. My PT Eve seemed to load me with enough tasks to take about 45 minutes to an hour. PTs know that if they load the patient too much, they won't do their tasks, so part of the PT art is figuring out what each patient will handle and what works best for them. Eve would always laugh when she gave me a new task, as she knew I would negotiate to eliminate something else from the list.

One challenge is to figure out ways to keep yourself motivated to do your assigned tasks. By my third shoulder surgery, my motivation came from knowing that if I didn't do the work, I wouldn't get the result.

Another motivation for me was to try to do my exercises first thing in the morning, particularly when I had advanced to only needing to do exercises once per day. I would also do the exercises I disliked the most first. The mental "trick" I was playing on myself was to get things out of the way early in the day so I felt good about getting the task done and I didn't fret about it hanging over my head.

Counting

In doing boring repetitive tasks like your PT exercises, it's easy to let your mind wander and lose count. One method I used to avoid this was to count in units of 10 and use objects in the room as markers. For example, 40 repetitions is a common exercise assignment. When doing my exercises in our hallway, I'd use the four door panels in our laundry room door for this purpose, shifting my vision to the one I just completed.

PT log

When I got new PT assignments, I'd write down the new assignments in my surgery logbook (kept in a computer document). This was usually done from memory, but I'd supplement it with the web-based service that Eve used to illustrate the exercises. It's good to review those every once in a while to ensure you're doing things correctly. The log is useful to make sure you don't forget something, as I'd print the log out and tape it to the wall for review the first few days of a new set of exercises. After a few days, it becomes a habit and you don't need the printout anymore.

I also kept track of my ROM and AROM measurements to send to my sister Debbie so she could keep up on how I was doing.

Showering

A priority is not to slip in the shower, as you'll have your sling off. Get a rubber mat for good traction or sit in a shower chair.

You'll have to soap yourself with one hand. Some people like pump soap for this, but I just use a bar of soap. The only area I can't wash easily is the armpit of the non-surgical shoulder. After a couple of weeks, I was able to just stick the bar of soap in the armpit with my surgical arm and was able to rub the arm around a bit to get things clean. It gets easier over time.

Drying off with one hand is challenging at first. I first squeegee myself off with my good hand to remove as much water as possible after turning the shower off. Then I dry myself with one hand. To do my back, I toss the towel over my back and rub against the door frame like a bear scratching against a tree. I put the toilet lid down and put a hand towel on top of the seat to dry my butt. I sit down and dry both legs with one hand. This works surprisingly well and there's no danger of slipping or losing your balance when bending over to dry your legs when standing (the older folks will understand this). You can also put your underwear on while sitting on the toilet lid.

Sling

The sling's purpose is to keep shoulder/arm immobilized so it can heal. Be careful while the nerve block is still blocking the pain to not accidentally bump and move the injured arm. Note the arm and hand will be paralyzed and you won't be able to move them until the block wears off. After the block wears off, you'll quickly learn you don't want to bump that arm because it hurts.

You will probably get a sling for your arm before the surgery. Try to get it a week or so before the surgery so you can practice with it.

This practice will be valuable. You will find out what it's like trying to live with only one functional hand/arm. Some daily tasks you'll need to do are pee, wipe, pull your pants up, get dressed, put socks and shirts on, put in your contacts and hearing aids, put on your makeup, comb your hair, use your phone and computer, eat, etc. This practice is doubly important if the arm getting the surgery is your dominant arm. I recommend wearing the sling for practice for at least 24 hours; 48 is even better. Put a sock over the sling hand to remind you that you are unable to use it. You'll of course cheat, but you'll find out after the surgery that your arm is either paralyzed or too painful to use, so the cheating will cost you some learning.

My first two surgeries were for my left (non-dominant) arm, but my third surgery was on my right arm. While I didn't wear the sling ahead of time, I wish I did, as it would have prepared me a little better.

One important reason to wear the sling constantly and firmly is to avoid blowing out the surgery. As pain levels drop, you'll probably want to get a little more casual about keeping the arm in the sling. Both my sister and the surgeon's PA have told me they've seen way too many people blow out their surgeries by not wearing their slings properly. An example of what can happen is they are carrying something and drop it, then react to catch it with the injured arm -- this happens fast because it's a reflex. Then they've strained those repairs that are supposed to be healing. Surgical outcomes after blowing out the first repair are much less successful than the original surgery. Another way of blowing the repair is tripping or falling and pulling the arm out of the sling and falling on it. I know I mentioned blowing out the repair before, but it's really worth repeating. Your surgeon, PA, and PT will all agree.

Note: with a violent event like tripping and falling, the sling may not protect you. A web page I read made the important point that the sling helps you reduce all the little motions and uses you'll make with that injured arm as it gets better. You say you won't, but it will terribly difficult not to, as your brain will reason that since it doesn't hurt, it's OK. But what you're doing is applying small repeated forces to the repairs and these can cause cumulative damage (it's called creep and can slowly enlarge the suture holes through the tendons).

Make sure the sling is adjusted properly. For my third surgery, the place I got the sling had a woman who knew exactly what to do and fitted the sling to me perfectly.

Small sling adjustments can make a big difference in pain levels in the first week after surgery. Eve's eye (my PT) spotted a problem the first time I saw her and she was instrumental in lowering my pain levels. On my third surgery, my wife's eye was good at looking at the sling and deciding on when an adjustment was needed (she had also worn a sling when she had broken her arm).

On my sling, there's a top cover that uses Velcro to secure your forearm into the sling. On the second day after surgery, I had stopped the opiate pain pills the night before, as I don't take them unless I really need them. My arm was in a lot of pain, particularly in my elbow area. This pain was similar to the nerve pain I get in the arm when having an MRI with an injured shoulder -- and after 25 minutes of laying perfectly still, this pain can reach level 6 or 7. The surgeon's nurse suggested taking the arm out of the sling while sitting in my chair and resting it on a pillow. When I opened up the sling, I found that it had been fastened far too tightly at the hospital and I had a large groove in my arm from the pressure of the sling. This relieved the pressure and things felt a lot better in a half hour or so. I keep this piece of Velcro open when I'm typing on my computer or loosely connected otherwise.

Later, I talked with Carol, the surgeon's PA and mentioned that for my first two surgeries, my sling was put on incorrectly with a twist in the back, causing a plastic ring to dig into my back. It wasn't horrible, but the discomfort increased over a couple of days. Carol told me the recovery room nurses were responsible for this stuff and often they don't do it because they're too busy. It doesn't really matter who's at fault -- it's important for you to be aware of fit problems and have someone knowledgeable about it to check things. I feel the PT is the best person for this task.

The sling hardware may bug you where it protrudes, especially if you sit for long periods. Get a thicker shirt or insert a pad or some foam. The plastic clips may poke you under certain conditions. On one sling there were some stiff fibers that would stick me sometimes and I used a razor blade to trim them. My last sling hasn't had any of these problems.

When sitting, you'll probably be allowed to remove the top clip of the sling to take your arm out and e.g. rest it on a pillow (this may be too painful in the first few days, but you'll find you really like doing it -- or just resting your arm stretched out). The sling design I have is poorly designed to get the clip reconnected, which you always want to do when you get out of the chair. If you're handy in the shop, there are aids that can be made to help with this and stiffen the floppy clips, but you'll want to do this before your surgery. This is where practicing wearing the sling a few days ahead of the surgery will pay off.

About a week after the surgery, I was able to lightly use the hand on the arm with the surgery. The surgeon's PA told me light-duty tasks at waist level were OK, but no lifting or weights more than 1 pound. You actually can take a load of a pound or a few pounds by putting the load in your hand and letting the sling support your wrist to take the load. But be careful, as it's too easy to make a mistake because you will probably use your shoulder muscles unconsciously.

Talk to your surgeon and PT about sling use. Search the web for advice too.

Because my sister and the surgeon's PA emphatically told me about the risks of blowing the surgery and emphasized keeping my arm in the sling for the first 6 weeks, I made a mental commitment to always keep the sling on if I wasn't in my recliner. You have to consciously make this commitment and remember it every time until it becomes habit, as you don't want to have that first accident and blow out the surgery.

You'll likely be a passenger in a car while in your sling. Here's what I learned to do while my wife and I went on a 7 hour trip: I put the lap part of the seat belt over my injured arm. This would trap my arm in a collision and hopefully would keep it from moving violently forward in a crash. This might mean the surgery doesn't get blown out. I don't know enough about traffic safety and crash mechanics to know whether this would be recommended or not by a knowledgeable engineer, but it makes sense in my mind and it's what I do.

Your surgeon will tell you how long you'll have to use the sling. For my three surgeries, the length was 6 weeks for each one. You won't be allowed to drive while you're supposed to be wearing the sling. If you do choose to drive, you may find your insurance company will not cover you in an accident because you went against doctor's orders.

For non-emergency driving, you can drive one-handed if you're careful (most of us do it a lot). The problem is that in an emergency, you may not be able to react quickly or assertively enough with one hand, particularly if the car doesn't have power steering. If you have to drive a lot after you're out of the sling and your surgeon tells you it's OK, you may want to get a written release from him to ensure you have insurance coverage in case of an accident.

Ice

Careful use of ice is important (something Debbie told me over and over). Get a good ice bag from your PT and have your PT show you how to safely use ice to reduce the discomfort of the surgery, swelling, or pain from exercising. The basic technique is to use cloth like a towel between the ice bag and your skin. Your basic objective is to not let the skin temperature get too cold. My technique was to use an IR thermometer and not let my skin temperature get below 20 degrees C. Ask your PT what their technique is.

I don't recommend the ice chests with an electric pump that circulate cold water through a shroud you put over your injured area. They are a pain to set up, 5 to 10 times more expensive than a good ice bag, and actually less effective (I had one a friend gave me and only used it a few times because it was a lot of work to get set up -- just what a surgical patient is not going to want to do). I remember the PT charged me $20 for an ice bag and it has been a superb tool worth far more than what it cost.

Overuse

It's common to start using your shoulder when you start to feel better, even when you're still in your sling. I had to learn the hard way not to do this. First, it can cause pain and it may not be obvious it's due to this use. Second, it can stress the sutures, causing loosening because you're gently damaging the non-healed tissues. The best strategy is to stay in the sling religiously for the first 6 weeks, both for protection from sudden movements and as a reminder that you shouldn't be moving that arm.
